osu! Hybrid Leaderboard

This leaderboard scores every player ranked in the union of the top-10k PP, Ranked Play, and OTR rankings by combining three measures (PP, Elo, and OTR), weighted near-equally on one shared scale. A player must carry at least one real Elo or OTR to appear. Low-playcount Elos are statistically shrunk toward the rating their PP predicts, and an OTR with few or no tournament matches behind it is down-weighted.

Formula used for this leaderboard:

score = 0.33 × z(log pp) + 0.34 × z(elo) + 0.33 × z(otr)

Each of the three axes is standardized across the board: z(x) = (x − mean) / std, measured over everyone on the board (PP is logged first because it is heavily skewed). That puts all three on the same standard-deviations-from-average scale, so a place gained on one axis is worth the same as on another — and the blend tracks magnitude, not just ordinal rank. The 0.33 / 0.34 / 0.33 split weights all three axes near-equally (only a hair more on Elo than raw PP) — these are the base weights, used when all three axes are real. OTR is a real tournament rating; players with no tournament history instead get an estimate seeded from their osu! rank (OTR's own starting formula), marked with a tilde (~). An estimated axis (a seeded OTR, or a PP-seeded Elo marked ^) carries no information of its own — a seeded OTR is essentially osu! rank in disguise — so it is dropped to zero weight and its share is redistributed to the player's real axes. That stops an estimate from quietly counting PP twice; a player with a real Elo but a seeded OTR is scored 0.49 · z(log pp) + 0.51 · z(elo). Zero weight here means only that the seed adds nothing to that player's own blend; the seeded value is still kept because each axis is standardized over the whole board, seeds included, so it helps set the average and spread every real rating is measured against. Blanking the seeds would shift that baseline and re-rank the board, which is why they stay. A real OTR is trusted in proportion to how much tournament play backs it — its weight scales as matches / (matches + 5), so a one- or two-match rating (barely nudged off its rank-seed) leans mostly on PP and Elo, while a deep tournament record earns close to its full share. The three vs columns (vs pp, vs elo, vs otr) show how many places a player gained (green ▴) or lost (red ▾) versus that one axis's rank alone (— when that axis is seeded, so there's no real rank to compare against). A big vs pp gain — tens of thousands of places — is signal, not a glitch: it flags a strong tournament or Ranked-Play player whose PP rank is deep only because they don't farm PP. The largest jumps are the most-confident competitive players (the board showing what PP misses); the genuinely low-confidence players sit near the bottom with much smaller deltas. An asterisk (*) next to a player's Elo marks a provisional rating — osu! considers it not yet stable because the player has too few recent Ranked Play matches. Click any column header to sort.

How low-play Elos are handled (shrinkage)

A ranked-play Elo built on a handful of matches is noisy. Rather than trust it blindly or throw the player out, each Elo is shrunk toward the rating their PP predicts, weighted by how many matches back it: elo = prior + (raw_elo − prior) × n / (n + 5), where prior is a PP→Elo fit (built only from stable, ≥10-match players) and n is the match count. With one match the Elo is pulled most of the way to its PP-expected value; by ~25 matches it is almost entirely the player's own. Because every Elo is adjusted this way, there's no per-row symbol for it — instead hover any Elo number to see its raw rating and match count. A provisional rating (osu! considers it unstable — too few recent matches) still carries an asterisk (*). A player with no Elo at all is just the n = 0 limit of the same formula (their Elo equals the prior), marked with a caret (^). The same shrunk value is what feeds the score, so the Elo column shows the adjusted rating, not the raw one.

Why the number 5? It is not arbitrary. Using OTR as an independent yardstick for competitive skill, a player's Elo only becomes a better skill predictor than their PP once they have about five ranked matches. Below five, a real Elo tracks OTR no better than a pure PP guess does (correlation 0.56 vs 0.56 — a statistical dead heat, Williams test p = 0.84); at five or more it pulls clearly ahead (0.70, Fisher z = 8.3, p < 10⁻¹⁵). Five is therefore where a match's worth of evidence equals the PP prior's — exactly the right half-weight point for the shrinkage above.

OTR is deliberately not shrunk like this — it doesn't need to be. Unlike osu!'s raw Ranked Play Elo, OTR is already a Bayesian rating seeded from osu! rank and tempered by its own volatility, so a low-tournament rating is already anchored near that prior internally. It is therefore used as-is — no extra shrinkage toward PP, which would only double-count that prior.

Biggest limitations

  • Elo is still inaccurate because too few players queue. For Elo to be meaningful, players — especially those at the top — need to play ranked matches relatively frequently. (This assumes the Elo system itself is reliable — it is brand new and still under active development.) The low-play shrinkage above softens this, but it cannot manufacture data that isn't there.
  • Shrinkage leans on an imperfect PP→Elo prior. Pulling a noisy Elo toward its PP-expected value assumes PP is a decent guess of competitive skill — but that fit is loose (it explains only about a third of the variance in Elo). So a genuine over-performer — someone far better in matches than their PP suggests — who has played very few matches will be tugged unfairly toward the crowd until they rack up games. Shrinkage trades a little of that edge-case fairness for a lot less small-sample noise.
  • The pools it draws from stop at 10,000. The board is the union of the PP top-10k, the Ranked Play top-10k, and the OTR top-10k — each leaderboard stops at 10k — so a player ranked outside all three pools never appears, even if their hybrid score would place them. Every hybrid rank is a standing within this union sample rather than a true global one. Adding the OTR pool now surfaces tournament-only players who don't grind PP or queue ranked play.
  • About a third of this board's OTR ratings are estimates, not real ones. OTR only rates players who have competed in verified tournaments — about two-thirds of the board. Everyone else gets a rating seeded from their osu! rank (marked ~), which is just OTR's starting prior, not evidence of actual tournament results. These seeded values are kept out of a player's own weighted blend (zero weight, redistributed to the player's real axes) precisely because they'd otherwise just re-count PP — they're still shown for context, so for those players the OTR column is shown for reference, but it is not inert: each axis is standardized over the whole board, so the seed still feeds that axis's mean and spread (the scale every real rating is judged against). A real OTR backed by only a handful of tournament matches sits just off that same seed, so it is partially down-weighted too — its share scales as matches / (matches + 5), and only a deep record earns its full weight.
  • OTR itself is a moving, partial target. It updates on a weekly cadence and decays after about six months of tournament inactivity, so a player's tournament axis can lag their current form. It also only counts approved matches — qualifiers, scrims and unverified events don't register.
  • The three-way weight split is debatable. The board uses base weights of 0.33 PP / 0.34 Elo / 0.33 OTR, weighting the three axes near-equally. That balance is a judgement call — a different split may be equally valid, or better. (Reliability weighting means a player missing a real axis is scored on the other two at the same relative ratio, rather than having a PP-derived placeholder diluting the blend.)
  • Normalization is relative to whoever is on the board. Each axis is standardized against this population, so a player's score shifts a little whenever the board's makeup changes. That is the trade for measuring magnitude on a common scale rather than blending raw, incomparable numbers — but it means scores are standings within this sample, not absolute values.

Does a hybrid leaderboard even need to exist?

If Elo were already an accurate representation of how players' skill levels compare, a hybrid leaderboard might not be needed at all. The two strongest arguments for its existence are:

  1. PP is a good gauge of raw mechanical skill, which is important in osu! and deserves to be accounted for.
  2. osu! is primarily a single-player experience. Even in tournaments, players never interact during gameplay — each one plays alone, and the winner is decided by comparing scores. By Chris Crawford's definition, that makes a tournament match a competition rather than a game (though it is, of course, still a video game). Since results come from each player's own performance and not from direct play against an opponent, a purely head-to-head rating like Elo can't tell the whole story by itself — so blending in PP, a measure of that individual performance, is justified. One could object that players do interact: they ban and pick beatmaps against their opponent in tournaments and ranked play. But that's a meta-game layered on top — the core gameplay loop, clicking circles to the beat for a high score, plays out in isolation and is unaffected by it.
