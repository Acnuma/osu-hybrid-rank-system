osu! Hybrid Leaderboard

This leaderboard scores every player ranked in the union of the top-10k PP, Ranked Play, and OTR rankings by combining three measures (PP, Elo, and OTR), weighted equally on one shared scale. A player must carry at least one real Elo or OTR to appear. Elo and OTR are both down-weighted when little play backs them — a low-match Elo or a thin-record OTR counts for less, so the score leans on the better-established measures.

Formula used for this leaderboard:

score = ( z(log pp) + z(elo) + z(otr) ) / 3

Each of the three axes is standardized across the board: z(x) = (x − mean) / std, measured over everyone on the board (PP is logged first because it is heavily skewed). That puts all three on the same standard-deviations-from-average scale, so a place gained on one axis is worth the same as on another — and the blend tracks magnitude, not just ordinal rank. The three axes carry equal weight — a third each; this is the base split, used when all three axes are real. OTR is a real tournament rating; players with no tournament history instead get an estimate seeded from their osu! rank (OTR's own starting formula), marked with a tilde (~). An estimated axis (a seeded OTR, or a PP-seeded Elo marked ^) carries no information of its own — a seeded OTR is essentially osu! rank in disguise — so it is dropped to zero weight and its share is redistributed to the player's real axes. That stops an estimate from quietly counting PP twice; a player with a real Elo but a seeded OTR is scored on roughly half z(log pp) + half z(elo). Zero weight here means only that the seed adds nothing to that player's own blend; the seeded value is still kept because each axis is standardized over the whole board, seeds included, so it helps set the average and spread every real rating is measured against. Blanking the seeds would shift that baseline and re-rank the board, which is why they stay. Each real axis is likewise trusted in proportion to the play behind it — a real Elo's weight scales as plays / (plays + 5) and a real OTR's as matches / (matches + 5) — so a thin record (barely off its ~PP seed) leans on the other axes, while a deep one earns close to its full share. The three vs columns (vs pp, vs elo, vs otr) show how many places a player gained (green ▴) or lost (red ▾) versus that one axis's rank alone (— when that axis is seeded, so there's no real rank to compare against). A big vs pp gain — tens of thousands of places — is signal, not a glitch: it flags a strong tournament or Ranked-Play player whose PP rank is deep only because they don't farm PP. The largest jumps are the most-confident competitive players (the board showing what PP misses); the genuinely low-confidence players sit near the bottom with much smaller deltas. An asterisk (*) next to a player's Elo marks a provisional rating — osu! considers it not yet stable because the player has too few recent Ranked Play matches. Click any column header to sort.

How low-play Elos are handled (reliability weighting)

osu!'s Ranked-Play "Elo" is not a raw number to be corrected — it is an OpenSkill posterior seeded from a PP estimate the first time you queue, then updated each match. So a low-match Elo already sits near that PP seed and drifts to the player's own level as games accrue — structurally the same object as OTR (seeded from rank). We therefore use the Elo at its own value and never edit it; instead its weight in the score scales with match count: weight = base × plays / (plays + 5). A one-match Elo counts little (the score leans on PP and OTR); by a few dozen matches it earns close to its full share. Because every Elo is weighted this way there's no per-row symbol — instead hover any Elo number to see the match count behind it. A player with no real Elo gets the PP-seed value at zero weight, marked with a caret (^); a provisional rating (osu! flags it unstable — too few recent matches) keeps its asterisk (*).

Does Elo even carry real skill signal — and why 5? Using OTR as an independent yardstick (it shares no data with Ranked Play or PP), a real Elo's agreement with OTR climbs with match count: a thin Elo barely beats a pure PP guess, but within a few matches it pulls clearly ahead (its Elo↔OTR correlation rises from about 0.5 to about 0.7). So Elo earns its place as an axis. The 5 in the weight taper, though, is a conventional reliability constant, not a derived threshold — in fact on the published board full weight is marginally the most accurate choice (a real Elo and PP are complementary, so down-weighting a thin Elo just leans on PP, which its seed already duplicates). The taper is a deliberate, standard hedge: it damps small-sample luck and gives a smooth ramp off the zero-weighted seed, applied the same way to Elo and OTR. None of this rests on trust — it is reproducible straight from the published leaderboard, and the repository ships the small script that recomputes the correlations and tests.

Elo and OTR are treated identically — because they are the same kind of thing. Both are OpenSkill posteriors seeded from a prior (Elo from a PP estimate, OTR from osu! rank) that washes out as play accrues. So neither rating's value is ever edited — we show each axis's own number — and all the reliability handling lives in the weight: a thin rating on either axis keeps its exact number but counts for less until enough play backs it, and a fully-seeded axis (no real play at all) gets zero weight. That symmetry is the whole point: there is no "raw Elo versus Bayesian OTR" distinction to justify handling them differently, because both are already Bayesian, seeded posteriors.

One honesty note: the same 5 is used for both axes by convention, not because each was independently pinned to that value. The data actually says the accuracy-optimal weight is close to full for both (the taper is a robustness hedge, not a tuned optimum), and there is no clean way to fit a separate reliability point per axis without circularity. So evidence / (evidence + 5) is a principled, conservative default — not a precisely-measured threshold.

Biggest limitations

  • Elo is still inaccurate because too few players queue. For Elo to be meaningful, players — especially those at the top — need to play ranked matches relatively frequently. (This assumes the Elo system itself is reliable — it is brand new and still under active development.) The reliability weighting above softens this, but it cannot manufacture data that isn't there.
  • Thin ratings lean on PP, which can under-sell a genuine over-performer. Down-weighting a low-match Elo (or OTR) shifts a player's score toward the axes that are well-backed — chiefly PP. So someone far better in matches than their PP suggests, with only a handful of games, is scored more conservatively than they deserve until they rack up play. Full weight would rank them more accurately on average, but at the cost of trusting genuine flukes; the reliability taper is the deliberately conservative choice.
  • The pools it draws from stop at 10,000. The board is the union of the PP top-10k, the Ranked Play top-10k, and the OTR top-10k — each leaderboard stops at 10k — so a player ranked outside all three pools never appears, even if their hybrid score would place them. Every hybrid rank is a standing within this union sample rather than a true global one. Adding the OTR pool now surfaces tournament-only players who don't grind PP or queue ranked play.
  • About a third of this board's OTR ratings are estimates, not real ones. OTR only rates players who have competed in verified tournaments — about two-thirds of the board. Everyone else gets a rating seeded from their osu! rank (marked ~), which is just OTR's starting prior, not evidence of actual tournament results. These seeded values are kept out of a player's own weighted blend (zero weight, redistributed to the player's real axes) precisely because they'd otherwise just re-count PP — they're still shown for context, so for those players the OTR column is shown for reference, but it is not inert: each axis is standardized over the whole board, so the seed still feeds that axis's mean and spread (the scale every real rating is judged against). A real OTR backed by only a handful of tournament matches sits just off that same seed, so it is partially down-weighted too — its share scales as matches / (matches + 5) (a thin real Elo is down-weighted the same way, by plays / (plays + 5)), and only a deep record earns its full weight.
  • OTR itself is a moving, partial target. It updates on a weekly cadence and decays after about six months of tournament inactivity, so a player's tournament axis can lag their current form. It also only counts approved matches — qualifiers, scrims and unverified events don't register.
  • The three-way weight split is debatable. The board uses equal base weights — a third each, weighting the three axes the same. That balance is a judgement call — a different split may be equally valid, or better. (Reliability weighting means a player missing a real axis is scored on the other two at the same relative ratio, rather than having a PP-derived placeholder diluting the blend.)
  • Normalization is relative to whoever is on the board. Each axis is standardized against this population, so a player's score shifts a little whenever the board's makeup changes. That is the trade for measuring magnitude on a common scale rather than blending raw, incomparable numbers — but it means scores are standings within this sample, not absolute values.

Does a hybrid leaderboard even need to exist?

If Elo were already an accurate representation of how players' skill levels compare, a hybrid leaderboard might not be needed at all. The three strongest arguments for its existence are:

  1. PP is a good gauge of raw mechanical skill, which is important in osu! and deserves to be accounted for.
  2. osu! is primarily a single-player experience. Even in tournaments, players never interact during gameplay — each one plays alone, and the winner is decided by comparing scores. By Chris Crawford's definition, that makes a tournament match a competition rather than a game (though it is, of course, still a video game). Since results come from each player's own performance and not from direct play against an opponent, a purely head-to-head rating like Elo can't tell the whole story by itself — so blending in PP, a measure of that individual performance, is justified. One could object that players do interact: they ban and pick beatmaps against their opponent in tournaments and ranked play. But that's a meta-game layered on top — the core gameplay loop, clicking circles to the beat for a high score, plays out in isolation and is unaffected by it.
  3. The three axes reward different habits, not the same skill three times. PP accrues almost passively — it counts whether or not a player ever touches multiplayer, and stops only if they deliberately opt out (unranked mods, or playing offline). Ranked Play is the competitive middle ground: quick, semi-random matches a few minutes long that reward skill without demanding much dedication. Tournaments sit at the structured end — pools announced in advance, time to prepare and strategize, matches drawn out over weeks. Because each axis rewards a different commitment, players skew toward one: a PP grinder who never enters a bracket, or a tournament specialist with a modest PP rank, is real skill that any single axis — Elo included — leaves on the table.
